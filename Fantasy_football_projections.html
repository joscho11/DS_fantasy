Brain storming: topic - fantasy football projections

I want to find statistical indicators of good fantasy football performance

what are stats that correlate to good fantasy football production?

This is a broad question. To start we will focus on Wide Recievers and Tight Ends, broadly known as pass catchers.

The type of league can have a huge impact on the fantasy output as well. The main 3 types of scoring format are standard, half points per reception, and full points per reception (PPR).

For standard, a fantasy football score is determined by yards and TDs. For half-PPR and full PPR leagues, a fantasy football score is determined by yards, TDs, and receptions.

This is an example for a WR/TE ex:

  • Standard - if a player has 3 receptions, 55 yards, and 1 touchdown they will have 5.5 (1 point per 10 yards) + 6 points (6 points per TD) = 11.5 points
  • Half PPR - if a player has 3 receptions, 55 yards, and 1 touchdown they will have 5.5 (1 point per 10 yards) + 6 points (6 points per TD) + 1.5 (half a point per reception) = 13 points
  • Full PPR - if a player has 3 receptions, 55 yards, and 1 touchdown they will have 5.5 (1 point per 10 yards) + 6 points (6 points per TD) + 3 (1 point per reception) = 14.5 points

Since all 3 types of scoring are widely used. I will use the middle ground for our data (half PPR).

Calculating the data myself will have a few flaws. First off, any obsecure way a player gained fantasy points will not be included (for example, scoring on a 2pt conversion, or scoring on a punt/kickoff return). In addition, the 18 week of the regular season is usually a very inaccurate week to look at for stats as many teams are not playing their starters 100% as they tank for the draft or get ready for the playoffs.

It is all very subjective, but I think calculating myself to remove outliers is the best way to have fair data.

It's very interesting to look at this data. In my opinion, TDs are very random in any given season, but heavily impact fantasy output. This is shown in the R^2 value of .65. It doesn't have an extremely strong correlation, although it does have some.

Overall, TDs should hold some weight for fantasy output, but it's really hard to know how much they can be used to predict future output.

For example, looking at an outlier above the regression line, you can see Diontae Johnson scored 131.7 half PPR fantasy points without scoring a single touchdown. Compare this to someone who was an outlier below the regression line like Chrisitian Watson. Watson scored 143.6 half PPR fantasy points but has 9 TDs that boosted his output heavily.

Some of the TD scoring can be attributed to random luck, but not entirely. Countless factors go into the likelihood a player scores a TD. A few factors that can have a large impact is 'big play' ability (yards per reception), the skill level of the pass catchers Quarterback, and the amount of redzone targets (pass thrown to them inside the 20 yard line) can show how likely a player is to score a TD.

After going through the first question, it will be easier to look at recievers and running backs separately (at least at first) because the stats differ quite widely between the two. The downside of doing this is the question of is a WR/TE or RB better more valuable for fantasy production will not be answered.
